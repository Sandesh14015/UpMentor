01
Context Engineering System™
CES — Patent-pending proprietary method

Most students give AI one-line prompts and get mediocre outputs. The CES teaches a structured, repeatable 5-layer method for building AI context that produces expert-level outputs every time. It is not prompt engineering — it is context architecture.

The 5 layers (proprietary): Layer 1: Role Assignment — who the AI is in this task
Layer 2: Problem Decomposition — breaking the task into atomic units
Layer 3: Constraint Specification — what the AI must and must not do
Layer 4: Output Scaffolding — what the output format and quality looks like
Layer 5: Iteration Protocol — how to refine based on output gaps

Students who learn CES produce outputs that look like they come from someone with 5 years of professional experience. That gap is visible. That gap is the credential.
02
Production Sprint™
PS — Proprietary project methodology

The Production Sprint™ is our proprietary 72-hour intensive where students take a real-world problem — from their school, their community, or an UpMentor employer partner — and produce a complete, deployable solution using AI tools. This is not a classroom exercise. It is real production.

What makes it unique: The problem must be real — provided by an employer partner or community institution
The student team must present to a real stakeholder, not a teacher
The output must be usable — a real deck, research report, tool, or campaign
The assessment is done by the stakeholder, not UpMentor staff

This is what earns the Internship Certificate. Not hours logged — real output accepted by a real client. No other program in India does this at school level.
03
Contribution Statement Protocol™
CSP — Proprietary ethical framework

The most dangerous thing about AI is that it makes plagiarism invisible and accountability disappear. The CSP is UpMentor's proprietary framework where every student documents, in structured form, what they contributed vs what AI contributed to every piece of work.

How it works: For every project output, students complete a Contribution Statement — a structured declaration that maps: what they decided, what they instructed AI to do, what they evaluated, what they rejected, and what they refined.

This trains intellectual accountability. It makes the student's thinking visible. It is what makes the UpMentor LOR trustworthy — because we can prove what the student actually did.

Employers love it. Colleges love it. No one else does it.

Phase 1 — AI Cognition · Weeks 1–3 · 9 Sessions
S1
The Real State of AI — What It Is, What It Isn't, What It's Becoming
Week 1 · 90 minutes · Foundation session
Wk 1
OBJECTIVE

Destroy myths. Install accurate mental models. Students leave knowing what AI can and cannot do at a technical level — not hype, not fear. Pure clarity.

Live demonstration: give the same task to GPT-4, Gemini, and Claude. Compare outputs. Students evaluate quality using a structured rubric (their first structured evaluation — this is the beginning of critical AI thinking)
Myth-busting exercise: 20 common AI beliefs. Students mark true/false, then we reveal with evidence. Creates cognitive dissonance — the starting point of real learning
The "AI Timeline" — students map what AI can do today vs 2020 vs projected 2030. Forces them to think about trajectory, not just current state
Output: "My AI Reality Map" — a personal one-page document of what they now understand about AI that they didn't before. Kept in their portfolio.
S2
How AI Actually Thinks — Tokens, Probability & Why It Hallucinates
Week 1 · 90 minutes · Technical intuition
Wk 1
OBJECTIVE

Build genuine technical intuition without maths. Students understand why AI does what it does — which is the foundation of knowing how to direct it effectively.

The "Autocomplete Game" — students predict what AI will write next, then compare. They begin to feel the probability nature of AI output. This intuition changes how they prompt forever.
Hallucination hunt: students are given AI-generated research with 5 deliberate errors. They find them using verification tools. This is the most important single exercise in the program — it teaches verification as instinct.
The "Confidence vs Accuracy" chart — students rate AI confidence on 10 responses, then fact-check them. The inverse correlation between confidence and accuracy is the key insight.
Output: Personal "AI Trust Calibration" — a framework each student writes for themselves on when to trust AI and when not to. Referenced throughout the program.
S3
Context Engineering System™ — Layer 1 & 2: Role + Decomposition
Week 1 · 90 minutes · CES Introduction — PROPRIETARY
Wk 1
™ PROPRIETARY · CES

Introduction to the Context Engineering System™. Students learn that the quality of AI output is not determined by the AI — it is determined by the quality of the context the human builds. This is the central philosophy of UpMentor.

CES Layer 1 — Role Assignment: Students learn to assign AI a specific expert identity before any task. The difference between "write me an essay" and "you are a senior research analyst at McKinsey — write me an essay" is demonstrated with live A/B outputs.
CES Layer 2 — Problem Decomposition: Students take a complex task and break it into 5–8 atomic subtasks. Each subtask gets its own AI instruction. Results are assembled. Students see that decomposed prompts produce professional-grade outputs vs single prompts produce generic outputs.
Live competition: two students, same task, one uses single prompt, one uses CES Layers 1+2. Outputs are shown to the class. Gap is always dramatic. This creates instant buy-in.
Output: CES Workbook entry — their first two-layer context structure for a task of their choosing. Saved to portfolio.
S4
Context Engineering System™ — Layer 3, 4 & 5: Constraint + Scaffold + Iterate
Week 2 · 90 minutes · CES Mastery — PROPRIETARY
Wk 2
™ PROPRIETARY · CES

Completing the full CES framework. Students now have a 5-layer method they own. By the end of this session, every student can produce outputs that are demonstrably superior to anything produced by someone who hasn't been through UpMentor.

CES Layer 3 — Constraint Specification: Students learn to tell AI what to avoid, what format to use, what length, what tone, what NOT to include. Constraints, paradoxically, produce more creative and accurate outputs.
CES Layer 4 — Output Scaffolding: Students create an explicit template of what the output should look like before asking AI to produce it. AI fills the scaffold. This produces outputs with consistent professional structure.
CES Layer 5 — Iteration Protocol: Students learn the evaluation loop — how to read AI output critically, identify specific gaps, and provide targeted refinement instructions. This is what turns a 7/10 output into a 9.5/10.
Output: Complete 5-layer CES document for a real assignment from their actual schoolwork. This is their first portfolio piece — produced in session, verified by trainer.
Assessment gate: students must demonstrate all 5 layers on a live task to a standard before moving to Phase 2. This is non-negotiable.
S5
AI for Research — Finding Truth in a World of Noise
Week 2 · 90 minutes · Applied cognition
Wk 2
OBJECTIVE

Students learn to use AI as a research multiplier — while maintaining rigorous verification. This session covers the research stack: AI-assisted search, source verification, triangulation, and structured synthesis.

The Research Stack: Perplexity AI for discovery → primary source verification → cross-referencing with 3 independent sources → AI-assisted synthesis → human editorial judgement. Students run through this stack on a real research question.
The "Dead Source" exercise: students are given 8 AI-generated citations. They verify each one. 3 are fake. The lesson: AI confidently invents sources. Verification is not optional.
Competitive research task: students research a real company in Odisha and produce a 1-page intelligence briefing using the research stack. Timed. Best briefing wins peer review recognition.
Output: Structured Research Brief on a topic of their choosing using the full research stack. Portfolio piece #2.
S6
AI Ethics, Deepfakes & the Responsibility Framework
Week 2 · 90 minutes · Ethics — the differentiator
Wk 2
OBJECTIVE

This is the session that separates UpMentor from every AI course that exists. We don't just warn students about risks — we install a decision framework they can apply to any AI situation for life.

The "AI Decision Matrix" — for any action involving AI, students evaluate across 4 axes: Is it accurate? Is it honest? Is it fair? Is it mine? Students apply this to 12 real scenarios — from using AI to write a college essay to generating a fake image for social media.
Deepfake recognition workshop: students see real deepfakes alongside real footage. They identify tells. This is increasingly important and no school teaches it.
The "AI Accountability Statement": for every AI output they produce in the real world, students learn to declare: what they asked, what AI produced, what they changed, what they verified. This is the foundation of the CSP™.
Introduction to Contribution Statement Protocol™ (CSP): students complete their first CSP for the research brief they produced in S5. This demonstrates that CES + CSP together make their work both powerful and honest.
Output: Personal "AI Ethics Commitment" — a one-page declaration they write and sign. This is referenced in their final LOR.
S7–9
AI Tools Mastery — The Complete Professional Stack
Week 3 · 3 sessions · Applied tool competency
Wk 3
OBJECTIVE

Three sessions covering the complete professional AI tools ecosystem. Not a tutorial — a structured competency build. Students are assessed on tool outputs, not tool knowledge.

Phase 2 — AI Production · Weeks 4–6 · 9 Sessions
S10
Subject-Specific AI — Science Stream: Hypothesis, Data & Research
Week 4 · 90 minutes · Subject integration
Wk 4
OBJECTIVE

AI integrated directly into what Science students are already studying. Not a separate AI lesson — a superpower for their existing curriculum. Students use AI to do science better, not to replace science.

Hypothesis generation with AI: students use CES to generate 10 research hypotheses for a biology/chemistry topic. They evaluate each hypothesis for testability, novelty, and feasibility. AI generates. Human evaluates. This is the correct division of labour.
Data interpretation: students feed real data from ISRO or WHO into AI and use it to find patterns, generate visualisations, and produce a written interpretation. This is what data science professionals do. Students do it in 90 minutes.
Literature review sprint: using Elicit + Consensus, students produce a 5-paper literature review on a topic of their choice in 45 minutes. Compare to how long this would take manually.

Subject-Specific AI — Commerce Stream: Financial Analysis & Business Models
Week 4 · 90 minutes · Subject integration
Wk 4
OBJECTIVE

Business idea evaluation: students pitch a business idea and use AI to produce a complete feasibility analysis — market size, competition, cost structure, revenue model. Compared to how a consulting firm would charge ₹5L for the same work.

S12
Subject-Specific AI — Arts/Humanities: Critical Analysis & Argument Construction
Week 4 · 90 minutes · Subject integration
Wk 4
OBJECTIVE

Arts students use AI not to write for them — to think harder than they ever could alone. AI as a Socratic sparring partner. The goal is stronger arguments, deeper analysis, more nuanced writing.

The "Devil's Advocate" exercise: students write a position on a current topic. They ask AI to argue the opposite as strongly as possible. They then revise their position. This produces the most intellectually honest, strongest arguments they've ever written.
Historical event reanalysis: students take an event from their history curriculum and use AI to find perspectives that are not in their textbook — indigenous voices, economic angles, regional impacts. AI expands what they thought they knew.
The "1000 Readers" exercise: students write a paragraph, then ask AI to roleplay as 5 different types of readers and describe their reaction. Students learn to write for audiences, not just for marks.
Output: Extended essay (800 words) on a topic of their choosing, with CSP documenting every AI interaction. The essay must demonstrate stronger argument construction than anything they've written before.
